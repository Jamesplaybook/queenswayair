Ontario summers are getting hotter, and an old, inefficient air conditioner isn't just uncomfortable — it's expensive. Modern high-SEER air conditioners use 20–50% less electricity than units from the early 2000s.

Here are 8 signs your central AC is ready for retirement.

## 1. It's More Than 12–15 Years Old

The average central air conditioner has a lifespan of 12–15 years with regular maintenance. If yours is approaching or past that age, you're living on borrowed time — especially if you've deferred tune-ups.

That doesn't mean you should replace a working unit the day it hits 13. But it does mean you should factor replacement into your planning rather than waiting for a failure on the hottest day of July.

## 2. You Need Frequent Repairs

If you've called an HVAC technician more than once in the last two years for your AC, the repair costs are probably adding up. A good rule of thumb: if the repair cost is more than **50% of the cost of a new unit**, replacement is usually the smarter financial choice.

## 3. It's Not Cooling Effectively

Warm air from the vents, uneven cooling between rooms, or a house that simply won't reach your set temperature — these are all signs that your system is no longer performing as designed. The cause might be low refrigerant, a failing compressor, or simply age-related degradation.

## 4. Your Energy Bills Are Creeping Up

If your electricity costs have been rising every summer (factoring out price increases), your AC's efficiency is declining. Compare your bills to what you paid 5–7 years ago. A significant increase in consumption on similar weather years is a red flag.

## 5. It Uses R-22 Refrigerant

R-22 (Freon) was phased out in Canada in 2020. If your older AC still uses R-22 and develops a refrigerant leak, you have two choices: replace the refrigerant at an increasingly high cost (supply is dwindling and expensive), or replace the unit. The decision is usually easy: replace the unit.

## 6. It's Making Strange Noises

Grinding, banging, rattling, or squealing sounds suggest mechanical issues — often a failing motor, loose components, or compressor problems. These issues are expensive to fix in older units. Get a technician's assessment, but be prepared for a replacement recommendation.

## 7. Humidity Control Is Poor

A properly functioning air conditioner dehumidifies your home as it cools. If your home feels clammy even when the AC is running, or condensation is forming on windows, the system may be oversized, undersized, or failing.

## 8. The Unit Has Significant Rust or Physical Damage

Surface rust on the unit's exterior isn't necessarily a problem, but rust on the coils, fan motor housing, or refrigerant lines can indicate deeper issues with moisture intrusion and component degradation.

## What to Look for in a New Air Conditioner

If any of the above signs apply, here's what to prioritize when shopping for a replacement:

- **SEER2 rating of 16+**: The minimum new standard in Canada. Higher is better for long-term energy savings.
- **Right sizing**: Don't just swap in the same size. A proper Manual J load calculation ensures you're not installing an oversized or undersized unit.
- **Variable-speed compressor**: More efficient, quieter, and better at humidity control than single-stage compressors.
- **Manufacturer warranty**: Look for 10-year parts + 5-year labour as a baseline.
